① HR Round

SECTION 1
Tell me about yourself +

What your sir means

This is the most important HR question. It's your opening statement — sets the tone. Most students ramble. You must be crisp, confident, and structured.

The 3-part formula:
1. Who you are (name, college, branch, year)
2. What you've done (projects, skills, achievements)
3. Why you're here (why this company/role excites you)

Practice actions

  • Write your answer first — keep it under 90 seconds
  • Record yourself speaking and listen back
  • Practice in front of a mirror until it feels natural
  • Mention 1 relevant project or skill you're proud of

Hobbies and interests +

What your sir means

Interviewers ask this to see your personality beyond academics. They want energy and authenticity. Never say "watching movies" or "listening to music" — everyone says that.

Golden rule: Pick a hobby you can talk about for 2 minutes without stopping. Relate it to skills — chess = strategic thinking, coding side projects = curiosity, reading = communication.

Practice actions

  • Prepare 2-3 genuine hobbies with a story or example for each
  • Practice linking each hobby to a skill employers value
  • Be ready for follow-up: "What did you last read?" or "Tell me more"

② Aptitude

SECTION 2
Arithmetic — speed and accuracy +

What your sir means

Percentages, profit/loss, ratios, time-speed-distance, averages. These appear in every aptitude test. Your sir wants you to solve these fast and without calculator.

Key trick: Learn multiplication tables up to 25, and percentage-fraction equivalents (1/8 = 12.5%, 1/6 = 16.67%, etc.). This cuts solving time by half.

Daily practice plan

  • Solve 10 arithmetic questions daily with a timer
  • Topics: percentages → profit/loss → time-speed-distance → averages
  • Target: each question under 90 seconds
  • Use: IndiaBIX or Freshersworld for free question banks

Logical reasoning — patterns and sequences +

What your sir means

Number series, letter patterns, blood relations, directions, coding-decoding. These test your ability to spot patterns quickly. There is always a hidden rule — find it.

Approach for series: Always check — difference, ratio, squares, cubes, and alternating patterns. If first attempt fails, try applying these in order.

Practice actions

  • Do 5 series + 5 coding-decoding questions daily
  • Practice blood relation and direction problems once per week
  • Time yourself: each question max 2 minutes
Clock problems — time → angle +

What your sir means

Given a time like 3:27, find the angle between the hour and minute hands. Your sir specifically mentioned this — it's a very common aptitude question category.

The core formula:
Minute hand moves: 6° per minute
Hour hand moves: 0.5° per minute
Angle = |11/2 × M − 30 × H| where H = hour, M = minutes

Example: 3:00 → |0 − 90| = 90°
Example: 6:00 → |0 − 180| = 180° (straight line)

Practice actions

  • Solve at least 15 clock problems using the formula until it becomes automatic
  • Also practice: when are hands coincident? perpendicular? opposite?
  • Coincident: every 65.45 minutes (11 times in 12 hours)
First thought approach (intuition first) +

What your sir means

Your sir wants you to think before picking up the pen. Write down what your first instinct says. Often your first thought is correct. This builds confidence and speed.

How to practice: Read the question → close eyes for 3 seconds → write what feels right → then verify. Train your gut. It will become reliable.

Practice actions

  • For each practice question: write your first thought first, then solve properly
  • Track how often your first thought was right — it'll improve over time
  • Great for MCQ tests where time is limited
Puzzles — Ants problem and no-formula problems +

What your sir means

The Ants problem: N ants on a pole, some going left, some right — how many fall off? How long does it take? Key insight: when two ants meet, they turn around — but it's equivalent to them passing through each other. This is a classic "think, don't calculate" problem.

The "ants on a pole" trick:
The time = the maximum distance any ant must travel to reach an end.
You don't need to track individual ants — just track how many ants end up at each side.

Other classic no-formula puzzles to practice

  • Bridge and torch problem — 4 people, different speeds, one torch
  • Egg drop problem — minimum tries to find the critical floor
  • Water jug problem — measure exact amount with odd-sized jugs
  • Weighing balance problems — find the odd coin in minimum weighings

③ Coding Round

SECTION 3 — Java & Python
Pattern programs +

What your sir means

Stars, numbers, pyramids, diamonds printed using loops. These test your loop logic and nested iteration understanding. Every fresher interview has at least one.

Think in 3 parts:
1. How many rows? → outer loop (i)
2. How many spaces? → inner loop for spaces
3. How many symbols? → inner loop for stars
Relation between i and number of stars = the pattern rule.

Must-practice patterns

  • Right triangle (stars increasing left to right)
  • Inverted triangle
  • Pyramid / Diamond (spaces + stars, symmetric)
  • Number pattern (Pascal's triangle)
  • Hollow square / hollow diamond
Arrays — set operations (union, intersection) +

What your sir means

Given two arrays, find their union (all unique elements from both) and intersection (only common elements). Your sir wants you to code this from scratch, without using built-in set libraries blindly.

Two approaches to know:
1. Brute force: nested loops, O(n²) — simple but slow
2. Using HashSet: O(n) — fast, preferred in interviews
Know both. Explain which you're using and why.

Practice actions

  • Code union and intersection in Java first, then Python
  • Handle duplicates: union should not repeat elements
  • Also practice: difference of two arrays (A - B)
  • Bonus: sort both arrays and use two-pointer method
Closest of two numbers +

What your sir means

Given an array and a target value, find the element closest to the target. This tests your understanding of absolute difference, sorting, and binary search.

3 approaches — know all:
1. Linear scan: track min |arr[i] - target|, O(n)
2. Sort + binary search: find insertion point, check neighbors, O(n log n)
3. For two arrays: sort one, binary search for each element of other

Practice actions

  • Solve for a single array first → then extend to two arrays
  • Handle tie case: if two elements are equally close, return the smaller one
  • Code in both Java and Python
HCF without built-in functions +

What your sir means

Find Highest Common Factor (GCD) of two numbers by writing the logic yourself — no math.gcd(), no library calls. Your sir wants to see you implement the algorithm.

Euclidean Algorithm (must know):
HCF(a, b) = HCF(b, a % b) until b = 0
Example: HCF(48, 18) → HCF(18, 12) → HCF(12, 6) → HCF(6, 0) = 6

LCM(a, b) = (a × b) / HCF(a, b)

Practice actions

  • Write iterative version of Euclidean algorithm
  • Write recursive version of same
  • Extend to find HCF of an array of numbers
  • Code LCM using your HCF function
Variable naming conventions +

What your sir means

Your sir is teaching you to write professional code, not just correct code. Variable names are the first thing an interviewer judges when reading your code.

Rules to follow:
Java: camelCase → studentCount, maxValue
Python: snake_case → student_count, max_value
Constants: ALL_CAPS → MAX_SIZE = 100
NEVER use: a, x1, temp2, abc

Practice actions

  • Go back to your previous programs and rename all bad variables
  • From now on: every variable name must describe what it stores
  • Short is ok — but descriptive: sum is fine, s is not
How to reduce conditionals (clean code) +

What your sir means

Instead of stacking 10 if-else blocks, write smarter code. Interviewers love candidates who write lean, readable logic. This shows maturity as a programmer.

Techniques to reduce if-else:
1. Use ternary: max = a > b ? a : b
2. Use dictionary/Map for condition → action mapping
3. Use early return: handle edge cases first and return, keep main logic clean
4. Use Math.max / Math.min instead of if-else comparisons
5. Merge conditions with && and || logically

Practice actions

  • Take any old program with 5+ if-else blocks → rewrite with fewer
  • Practice writing grade calculator without if-else chains (use dict/map)
  • Practice FizzBuzz in multiple ways — try without any if statements

④ Review Habits

SECTION 4
Do you have any questions? (ask back) +

What your sir means

At the end of every interview, you'll be asked: "Do you have any questions for us?" Saying "No" is a missed opportunity. Your sir wants you to always have 2 smart questions ready.

Good questions to ask:
• "What does a typical day look like for someone in this role?"
• "What skills does your top performer in this team have?"
• "What are the learning and growth opportunities here?"
Never ask about salary in the first round.
After each session: what mistakes did you make? +

What your sir means

This is the most important study habit. Your sir ends with this for a reason — improvement only comes from knowing where you went wrong. Not just moving on.

The 3-question review after every practice session:
1. What did I get wrong? Write it down.
2. Why did I get it wrong? (Concept gap? Careless? Time pressure?)
3. How will I avoid this next time?
